ABSTRACT

This report presents the design, implementation, testing, and regulatory compliance analysis of a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) iOS mobile application developed for SE4020 — Mobile Application Design and Development at the Sri Lanka Institute of Information Technology (SLIIT). The application, NurseryConnect Keyworker Module, targets the Keyworker (Early Years Practitioner) role within a UK-regulated early years childcare setting based on the NurseryConnect case study for Little Stars Nursery & Daycare.

The application was developed entirely in Swift using SwiftUI, adopting the Model-View-ViewModel (MVVM) architectural pattern with the iOS 17 @Observable macro for reactive, dependency-tracked state management. The project comprises 49 Swift source files, approximately 13,815 lines of code, 18 distinct screens, and 12 reusable UI components. Two core features are implemented within the four-week MVP scope: (1) Daily Diary and Activity Monitoring, enabling EYFS-aligned structured logging of child welfare activities; and (2) Incident Reporting and Safeguarding Workflow, providing a RIDDOR-aligned digital incident management system with body map injury recording, auto-timestamping, and simulated compliance lifecycle management.

The implementation demonstrates a range of advanced SwiftUI and iOS development techniques: glassmorphism and neumorphism visual effects via custom ViewModifiers, animated circular ProgressRing components using AngularGradient and spring animations, a three-page parallax onboarding flow, a custom body map with Path-drawn body silhouettes and normalised tap coordinate capture, a Combine-powered urgency timer, centralised HapticManager abstraction, and a dual-theme system with animated dark/light mode transitions.

The regulatory compliance chapter critically analyses all five applicable frameworks — UK GDPR, EYFS 2024, Children Act 1989, Ofsted EIF, and FSA guidelines — identifying four genuine tensions between regulatory requirements and real-world usability with evidence-based mitigations. Testing was conducted through eleven structured manual test scenarios; three specific bugs were identified, diagnosed, and resolved during development. The report concludes with a prioritised five-point future work plan for the production deployment phase.

Keywords:  SwiftUI, iOS 17, MVVM, @Observable, Glassmorphism, Childcare Management, UK GDPR, EYFS 2024, RIDDOR, Incident Reporting, Daily Diary, Compliance by Design, NurseryConnect.

1  INTRODUCTION
1.1  Background
The United Kingdom early years childcare sector operates within one of the most complex and demanding regulatory environments in public service provision. Early years settings are simultaneously bound by the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) Statutory Framework 2024, the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) and Data Protection Act 2018, the Children Act 1989, and the Ofsted Education Inspection Framework. Collectively, these frameworks require accurate, timely, and auditable records of child welfare activities — including daily activity diaries, incident reports, developmental assessments, and attendance logs — while placing stringent limitations on how child data may be stored, shared, and retained.

Current practice in many UK early years settings relies on paper-based forms and informal digital tools such as WhatsApp groups. The NurseryConnect case study, prepared for Little Stars Nursery & Daycare, documents these operational failures comprehensively: paper-based incident reporting creates audit trail vulnerabilities, unregulated photo sharing constitutes a GDPR violation, and the absence of role-based access control breaches the data minimisation principle. The NurseryConnect system is proposed as a multi-role, cloud-based replacement addressing these deficiencies across eight distinct user roles.

The SE4020 Assignment 1 task requires the development of a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) iOS application targeting one selected user role from the case study, implementing exactly two primary features within a four-week individual development timeline. This report presents the complete design, implementation, and compliance analysis of the NurseryConnect Keyworker Module — a native iOS application developed in Swift and SwiftUI comprising 49 source files and approximately 13,815 lines of code.
1.2  Chosen User Role: Keyworker (Early Years Practitioner)
The Keyworker role was selected as the perspective for this application. Within the NurseryConnect system architecture, the Keyworker is the most operationally intensive daily role: responsible for logging all child observations, recording incidents, managing meal and nappy entries, conducting wellbeing checks, and maintaining parent communication for their assigned children. This role was chosen for three specific, evidence-based reasons:
	•	Operational centrality — the Keyworker generates the highest daily data entry volume, providing the richest opportunity to demonstrate form-heavy workflows, type-specific data models, validation, persistence, and navigation depth within the MVP scope.
	•	Safeguarding significance — the combination of daily diary and incident reporting features serves the most critical compliance obligations of EYFS 2024 (welfare documentation and same-day notification) and Children Act 1989 (safeguarding records and escalation), enabling meaningful compliance-by-design decision-making to be demonstrated.
	•	Technical diversity — implementing structured data entry (diary) alongside a compliance-driven workflow (incident reporting) within a single role context showcases a broader range of SwiftUI component types, architectural patterns, and advanced concepts than a single-feature approach.
1.3  Selected Features and Justification
The two features implemented in this MVP are: (1) Daily Diary and Activity Monitoring, aligned with case study functional requirements FR-13 to FR-18; and (2) Incident Reporting and Safeguarding Workflow, aligned with FR-24 to FR-29.

These two features are complementary within the Keyworker's role context: a shift begins with diary logging and may escalate to incident reporting when a welfare concern arises. Together they represent a coherent, realistic four-week scope for five reasons: (a) they share the same ChildProfile data model, reducing duplication and demonstrating clean model design; (b) both require form-heavy UX with type-specific validation — a significant engineering effort appropriate for a four-week timeline; (c) both carry explicit regulatory obligations that can be meaningfully addressed in the compliance report; (d) neither requires a backend service to demonstrate its core value; and (e) together they cover both read and write operations, both simple and complex navigation flows, and both synchronous and time-driven UI behaviour.

Login, authentication, and access control were explicitly excluded as selected features per assignment requirement 1. The application launches directly into its functional flow via an animated splash screen and a three-page onboarding sequence.
1.4  Problem Statement
The Keyworker role faces four operationally significant challenges that the NurseryConnect Keyworker Module directly addresses:
	•	Incomplete and inconsistent daily records created via paper forms risk non-compliance during Ofsted inspections, which audit the completeness of welfare documentation against EYFS requirements.
	•	Delays in incident reporting via retrospective paper completion risk breaching the EYFS same-day parent notification obligation, creating potential legal exposure for the setting.
	•	Data privacy violations occur when child welfare information is communicated through unencrypted channels such as WhatsApp, which fails GDPR's integrity and confidentiality principle.
	•	Significant administrative burden during busy care periods reduces practitioner availability for direct child interaction — the core purpose of their role.
1.5  Objectives
	•	Design and implement a functional iOS MVP application targeting the Keyworker role, targeting iOS 17 and above using Swift and SwiftUI exclusively.
	•	Implement the Daily Diary and Activity Monitoring feature with type-specific entry forms covering six entry types: activities, sleep, meals, nappy changes, wellbeing checks, and general notes.
	•	Implement the Incident Reporting and Safeguarding Workflow with a RIDDOR-aligned form, interactive body map, auto-timestamping, and simulated compliance lifecycle management.
	•	Demonstrate clean MVVM architecture with iOS 17 @Observable state management, NavigationStack navigation, and a reusable component library including glassmorphism and neumorphism design patterns.
	•	Embed compliance-aware design decisions across architecture and UI reflecting UK GDPR, EYFS 2024, Children Act 1989, Ofsted EIF, and FSA guidelines.
	•	Validate all critical functional flows through structured manual testing and document outcomes honestly, including identified bugs and their resolutions.
1.6  Scope and Boundaries
This project is bounded as follows: iOS platform only, targeting iOS 17+; Swift 5.9 and SwiftUI exclusively; two features from the Keyworker role perspective; local JSON persistence via Codable and UserDefaults (no backend, API, or cloud service); simulation of manager countersign and parent notification workflows within the app; and no login or authentication implementation per assignment requirement 1.
2  LITERATURE REVIEW AND REGULATORY COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS
2.1  Mobile Applications in Professional Care Settings
Research in electronic health records adoption consistently demonstrates that structured digital forms with mandatory field validation and immediate user feedback significantly reduce documentation errors compared to paper-based systems (Boonstra & Broekhuis, 2010). The childcare sector shares many of these documentation obligations, particularly regarding safeguarding records and consent management. Commercial solutions in the UK nursery sector — including Tapestry, Famly, and iConnect — address these needs as full-platform multi-role systems, but there is limited published research examining role-specific tool design for Early Years Practitioners specifically.

The design patterns adopted in NurseryConnect Keyworker Module — type-specific adaptive forms, mandatory field validation with toast-based feedback, progressive disclosure via modal sheets, and fast-entry FAB patterns — are grounded in Apple's Human Interface Guidelines (Apple, 2024) and reflect evidence that usability-first design significantly improves practitioner adoption rates in high-pressure care environments (Holden & Karsh, 2010). The glassmorphism and neumorphism visual effects used in the application additionally reflect current iOS design trends established in iOS 15+ applications, providing a professional, contemporary aesthetic appropriate for the childcare-professional context specified in assignment requirement 5.

2.2.2  Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) Framework 2024
The EYFS Statutory Framework mandates accurate welfare record-keeping, keyworker accountability for assigned children, and same-day parent notification for all accidents and injuries. The daily diary module directly fulfils the welfare documentation obligation, providing six structured entry types aligned to daily childcare workflows. EYFS area tagging in activity log entries (linking recorded activities to EYFS areas of learning) reflects the developmental tracking obligation. The incident reporting module's same-day notification reminder banner — displayed prominently on any incident where parent notification has not been confirmed — directly implements the notification timeline obligation. The onboarding flow's third screen, titled 'Built for Compliance', surfaces GDPR, EYFS, and Ofsted branding, demonstrating the application's compliance-aware positioning to keyworkers from first use.
2.2.3  Children Act 1989 and Safeguarding
The Children Act 1989 mandates secure and confidential child records, clear escalation procedures for safeguarding concerns, and Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) accountability. The incident reporting module reflects these requirements in five specific design decisions: (1) six safeguarding-specific incident categories including a dedicated 'Safeguarding Concern' type in IncidentCategory enum; (2) mandatory immediate action documentation as a required field with non-empty validation; (3) an in-form EYFS Safeguarding Procedure alert presented automatically when a safeguarding category is selected, citing Children Act obligations and DSL contact; (4) body map recording for accurate, defensible injury location documentation; and (5) non-editable auto-timestamp at form open (Date()) which protects record integrity against retrospective alteration — a direct legal safeguarding mechanism.
2.2.4  Ofsted Education Inspection Framework
Ofsted inspects nurseries against the Education Inspection Framework, reviewing safeguarding record quality, documentation completeness, staff-to-child ratio compliance, and parental engagement evidence. The incident list view's status badges, urgency timers, and chronological grouped display (Today / This Week / Earlier) directly serve inspection readiness by ensuring no incident is invisible or ambiguously dated. The daily diary's timeline-based display and daily summary screen provide the structured evidence base Ofsted inspectors review for welfare documentation completeness. The end-of-day checklist feature (EndOfDayChecklistView) provides structured shift-end documentation aligned with inspection evidence requirements.
2.2.5  Food Standards Agency (FSA) Guidelines
For meal-related records, FSA guidelines for early years settings require allergen awareness and nutritional documentation. The application's meal diary entries capture food offered, food consumed (on a five-point PortionConsumed enum: all / most / half / little / none / refused), drink type, and drink volume in millilitres. Child profiles include allergens (using the 14 EU/UK major allergen categories modelled in the Allergen struct with AllergenSeverity: intolerance / allergy / anaphylactic), dietary requirements, and medical conditions. The UI surfaces allergen and dietary indicators in the child profile view. A full FSA-compliant meal planning module is deferred to future development; however, the Allergen data model and meal entry schema are designed to support it without schema changes.

The most significant unresolved tension in the MVP is the absence of a backend security layer. UK GDPR Article 25 requires data protection by design and by default, which in a production system demands AES-256 encryption at rest, TLS 1.3 in transit, database-level row isolation (keyworkers querying only their assigned children's data), and a formally documented DPIA prior to any processing of real child data. The MVP's local UserDefaults persistence does not meet this standard. This is acknowledged as a deliberate, transparent scope decision: the MVP demonstrates compliance-aware architecture design and data modelling while deferring infrastructure compliance to the production phase. A production deployment would also require appointment of a Data Protection Officer (DPO) and ICO registration as a data controller.
3  METHODOLOGY AND SYSTEM DESIGN
3.1  Development Methodology
The project adopted an iterative, feature-driven development approach aligned with Agile principles, adapted for individual four-week development. Development was structured into four sequential phases: Phase 1 — Architectural scaffolding (project structure, MVVM layer definitions, theme system, colour assets, utility extensions, and data models including all enums and Codable structs); Phase 2 — Daily Diary module (DataManager with diary CRUD, DiaryViewModel, DiaryEntryFormView with all six entry type form groups, DailyDiaryView timeline, DailySummaryView, and FAB integration); Phase 3 — Incident Reporting module (IncidentViewModel, IncidentFormView with body map and EYFS safeguarding alert, IncidentListView with search/filter/grouping, IncidentDetailView, and status lifecycle simulation); Phase 4 — Integration and polish (onboarding, splash animation, custom tab bar, NeumorphicCard and GlassCard components, ToastBanner, empty states, and Dark Mode validation).

Validation logic was developed alongside each form rather than as a separate phase, catching edge cases during initial feature development. Each screen was validated in the SwiftUI @Preview system in multiple data states before integration into the NavigationStack. The project was version-controlled using Git throughout development.

3.2.1  State Management: iOS 17 @Observable
The iOS 17 @Observable macro is used throughout the application. It provides automatic, fine-grained dependency tracking: SwiftUI only re-renders the specific views that read a changed property, rather than re-rendering all views observing an ObservableObject as in the legacy pattern. All five service classes (DataManager, AttendanceManager, MessageManager, NotificationManager, SleepTrackerManager), all three ViewModels, and AppState are @Observable. To create SwiftUI bindings from @Observable objects in view bodies, the @Bindable property wrapper is used: @Bindable var bindableAppState = appState. This pattern — required because @Observable does not expose $ projected values by default — was carefully implemented following Apple's iOS 17 migration documentation.
3.2.2  Data Persistence Design
DataManager implements a five-key UserDefaults store with JSONEncoder (ISO 8601 date encoding) and JSONDecoder (ISO 8601 date decoding). All domain models conform to Codable with explicit CodingKeys where needed. The service layer is the sole persistence owner — ViewModels call DataManager CRUD methods and receive model values, never interacting with UserDefaults directly. DataManager also implements a SampleData fallback: if no persisted data is found (first launch), pre-populated sample children, diary entries, and incidents are loaded, providing an immediately demonstrable application state.

3.5  Navigation Architecture
The application uses a custom SwiftUI tab bar (CustomTabBar, ~180 lines) with five destinations: Dashboard, Daily Diary, Quick-Add (FAB), Incidents, and Settings. NavigationStack is used within each tab for push-based in-stack navigation without the deprecated UINavigationController dependency. Modal workflows are presented as SwiftUI sheets with .presentationDetents([.large]) and .presentationDragIndicator(.visible).

A key navigation design decision is the FAB quick-log pattern coordinated via AppState. When a keyworker taps the FAB and selects an entry type, AppState.triggerFAB() sets the entry type and evaluates whether a child is currently selected in context. If selected, showFABSheet = true presents the form immediately. If no child is selected, showChildPickerFirst = true presents a lightweight child picker sheet first; upon selection, a 300ms DispatchQueue.main.asyncAfter delay prevents SwiftUI's simultaneous sheet presentation conflict before showFABSheet is set. This pattern required careful state management and demonstrates understanding of SwiftUI's single-active-sheet presentation constraint.

4.2  Launch Sequence and Entry Point
The application entry point (Assignment1App.swift) manages the full launch sequence using @State variables and UserDefaults persistence. On first launch, SplashView is displayed with a multi-stage animation sequence, followed by OnboardingView — a three-page TabView with page indicators and animated transitions presenting the app's two features and compliance positioning. Code Listing 1: SplashView Animation Sequence (SplashView.swift)
private func startAnimations() {
    // Logo scale & rotation with spring physics
    withAnimation(.spring(response: 0.8, dampingFraction: 0.6)) {
        scaleEffect = 1.0
        rotationAngle = 0
    }
    // Floating circles — infinite repeat
    withAnimation(.easeInOut(duration: 2)) { isAnimating = true }
    // Hue-rotating gradient background — infinite repeat
    withAnimation(.linear(duration: 4).repeatForever(autoreverses: true)) {
        gradientOffset = 30
    }
    // Title reveal with delayed spring
    withAnimation(.spring(response: 0.6, dampingFraction: 0.8).delay(0.5)) {
        showTitle = true
    }
    // Tagline + nursery name
    withAnimation(.easeOut(duration: 0.5).delay(1.0)) { showTagline = true }
    // Auto-dismiss after 2.5 seconds
    DispatchQueue.main.asyncAfter(deadline: .now() + 2.5) {
        withAnimation(.easeInOut(duration: 0.5)) { isFinished = true }
    }
}

4.3  AppState: Cross-Boundary Navigation Coordinator
AppState is the most architecturally significant class in the application. As an @Observable class, it serves as the centralised coordinator for all global sheet presentations and the FAB navigation flow, preventing the common SwiftUI anti-pattern of multiple views competing to present sheets simultaneously.

@Observable
final class AppState {
    // FAB navigation flags

struct FormValidator {
    static func isNotEmpty(_ text: String) -> Bool {
        !text.trimmingCharacters(in: .whitespacesAndNewlines).isEmpty
    }
    static func minLength(_ text: String, length: Int) -> Bool {
        text.trimmingCharacters(in: .whitespacesAndNewlines).count >= length
    }
    static func validateIncidentForm(
        description: String, location: String,
        actionTaken: String, category: IncidentCategory?
    ) -> [String] {
        var errors: [String] = []
        if category == nil {
            errors.append("Please select an incident category")
        }
        if !isNotEmpty(description) {
            errors.append("Description is required")
        }
        if !minLength(description, length: 10) {
            errors.append("Description must be at least 10 characters")
        }
        if !isNotEmpty(location) { errors.append("Location is required") }
        if !isNotEmpty(actionTaken) {
            errors.append("Immediate action taken must be recorded")
        }
        return errors
    }
}

4.8  Advanced SwiftUI Concepts Demonstrated
4.8.1  Animated ProgressRing Component
ProgressRing (ProgressRing.swift) implements a circular progress indicator using SwiftUI's Circle().trim() with an AngularGradient stroke. A @State private var animatedProgress drives a spring animation on .onAppear, creating a smooth fill-up effect. The MiniProgressRing variant uses lineWidth: 4, size: 36 for inline dashboard use. This demonstrates custom Shape-based animation without third-party libraries.

Code Listing 7: ProgressRing Animated Component (ProgressRing.swift)
struct ProgressRing: View {
    let progress: Double  // 0.0 to 1.0
    var color: Color; var lineWidth: CGFloat; var size: CGFloat
    @State private var animatedProgress: Double = 0
 
    var body: some View {
        ZStack {
            // Background track
            Circle().stroke(color.opacity(0.15), lineWidth: lineWidth)
            // Animated fill with AngularGradient
            Circle()
                .trim(from: 0, to: animatedProgress)
                .stroke(AngularGradient(
                    colors: [color.opacity(0.6), color],
                    center: .center,
                    startAngle: .degrees(0),
                    endAngle: .degrees(360 * animatedProgress)),
                    style: StrokeStyle(lineWidth: lineWidth, lineCap: .round))
                .rotationEffect(.degrees(-90))
            Text("\(Int(animatedProgress * 100))%")
                .font(.ncMono(size * 0.22)).fontWeight(.bold)
        }
        .frame(width: size, height: size)
        .onAppear {
            withAnimation(.spring(response: 1.0,
                dampingFraction: 0.8).delay(0.3)) {
                animatedProgress = min(progress, 1.0)
            }
        }
    }
}

4.8.4  HapticManager: Centralised Haptic Abstraction
HapticManager provides a centralised static abstraction over UIKit's UIImpactFeedbackGenerator and UINotificationFeedbackGenerator. Three patterns are used consistently: .lightTap() for standard interactions (tab switching, child selection, toggle), .mediumTap() for confirmations (form saves, status changes), and .error() for validation failures. This consistency in haptic language provides an additional accessibility channel alongside visual feedback, and ensures haptic feedback does not accidentally intensify for error states on the first test of a new form field.
4.8.5  Dynamic Colour System and Dark Mode
The application implements a complete dual colour system with custom named colours prefixed nc (ncPrimary, ncSecondary, ncBackground, ncBackgroundDark, ncText, ncTextSecondary, ncSuccess, ncWarning, ncError, ncAccent, ncGradientEnd). All colours are defined as dynamic colour assets in Assets.xcassets with separate light and dark appearance values. ThemeManager (@Observable) exposes isDarkMode with UserDefaults persistence and a withAnimation(.easeInOut(duration: 0.3)) toggle() method applied at the root level via .preferredColorScheme(). This provides smooth, animated light/dark mode transitions across the entire application hierarchy from a single state change.

5.3  Debugging Practices and Bug Resolutions
Three specific bugs were identified, diagnosed, and resolved during development, demonstrating active and methodical debugging practice:


Symptom: Selected tab icon appeared with incorrect tint in dark mode. Diagnosis: Hardcoded UIColor used in tab bar item configuration. Resolution: Replaced with dynamic ncPrimary asset colour defined with separate light/dark appearance values in Assets.xcassets. Detection method: @Preview with explicit .preferredColorScheme(.dark).


Symptom: Opening the form for a second entry of the same type showed values from the previous entry. Diagnosis: prepareNewEntry(type:) was setting the entry type but not explicitly resetting all ViewModel form fields. Resolution: Added comprehensive resetFormFields() call at the start of prepareNewEntry(type:) before setting the new type. Detection method: Rapid sequential FAB taps during manual testing.


Symptom: Elapsed time on incident cards froze after app was backgrounded and returned to foreground. Diagnosis: Timer.publish stream paused during background; .onReceive continued observing a paused stream. Resolution: Added .onReceive(NotificationCenter.default.publisher(for: UIApplication.willEnterForegroundNotification)) to trigger a manual timer state refresh on foreground re-entry. Detection method: Background/foreground cycling during manual testing.

6  CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
6.1  Conclusion
This project has delivered a functional, architecturally sound, and compliance-aware iOS MVP application for the NurseryConnect Keyworker role. The two implemented features — Daily Diary and Activity Monitoring and Incident Reporting and Safeguarding Workflow — represent a coherent, operationally realistic four-week scope directly addressing the Keyworker's most critical daily responsibilities and the most significant compliance obligations of EYFS 2024, Children Act 1989, and UK GDPR.

The implementation demonstrates iOS development at the current state of the art: iOS 17 @Observable macro, NavigationStack push navigation, adaptive @ViewBuilder form composition across six entry types, a 12-component reusable UI library including glassmorphism and neumorphism design patterns, an animated ProgressRing with AngularGradient and spring physics, an interactive body map with normalised Path-based zone coordinates, Combine-powered urgency timer, centralised HapticManager, multi-stage animated onboarding, and a dual-theme system with animated Dark Mode transitions. The project comprises 49 Swift files and approximately 13,815 lines of code.

The regulatory compliance analysis goes beyond surface-level description: four genuine tensions between regulatory requirements and real-world usability are identified (form completeness vs speed, timestamp integrity vs flexibility, data minimisation vs operational richness, and consent granularity vs user burden), with evidence-based mitigations proposed for each. The absence of a production-grade security layer is acknowledged with full transparency and a clear, prioritised remediation path.

The primary quality gap is the absence of automated XCTest and XCUITest coverage, which is openly acknowledged as the first remediation priority before any production deployment. Three specific bugs identified during development were diagnosed and resolved, demonstrating active debugging engagement. The application is ready for class demonstration and provides a solid, extensible foundation for the production NurseryConnect system.
6.2  Future Work
6.2.1  Automated Test Coverage — Highest Priority
A comprehensive XCTest unit test suite for DiaryViewModel and IncidentViewModel validation logic, combined with XCUITest UI tests for all critical user flows, is the highest-priority next step. The service layer abstraction enables ViewModel unit tests using mock DataManager implementations without requiring a running persistence layer. Target coverage: 80% of all ViewModel and Service code paths.
6.2.2  Backend Integration and Multi-User Synchronisation
Replacing UserDefaults persistence with a REST API backend aligned with the NurseryConnect microservices architecture (Node.js + NestJS backend, PostgreSQL for structured data, MongoDB for diary logs, AWS infrastructure per case study) would enable real-time multi-device synchronisation, genuine manager countersign workflows via push notification, and actual parent notification delivery. The service layer abstraction makes this migration achievable with changes confined to the service layer only.
6.2.3  Authentication and GDPR Security Infrastructure
Implementing FR-01 to FR-07 — MFA, biometric authentication via LocalAuthentication framework (Face ID / Touch ID), and session timeout — is the highest-priority production security requirement. Alongside this, AES-256 encryption at rest, TLS 1.3 in transit, database-level row isolation (keyworkers querying only assigned children), DPIA documentation, DPO appointment, and automated data retention (child records until age 25; incident records until age 21; diary entries 3 years post-departure) are required for legal compliance under UK GDPR.
6.2.4  EYFS Developmental Tracking
Extending child profiles to include EYFS developmental progress tracking against the seven areas of learning (Communication and Language; Physical Development; Personal, Social and Emotional Development; Literacy; Mathematics; Understanding the World; Expressive Arts and Design) with milestone recording and evidence attachment would complete the developmental monitoring capability described in the case study and significantly enhance inspection readiness.
6.2.5  Offline-First Architecture with Conflict Resolution
The non-functional requirements specify offline diary logging with sync on reconnection. Implementing Core Data as a local cache with a conflict resolution strategy (last-write-wins for diary entries; server-authoritative for incident records) would satisfy this requirement and significantly improve reliability in settings with intermittent connectivity.
